Review paper in *Nature Materials* Vol. 6 (November 2007), pp. 824–832. Co-authored by Wuttig (RWTH Aachen) and Yamada (Matsushita Electric/Panasonic), two of the foundational figures in PCM research.

## Key Claims

- All optical-storage PCMs share an **octahedral-like (p-bonded) crystal structure** — rocksalt for GeSbTe, simple cubic for Te-based alloys, distorted cubic for AIST.
- **Amorphous state**: Ge atoms are tetrahedral; on crystallization they "flip" to octahedral via Kolobov's "umbrella flip." Even-folded ring structures in amorphous GST preserve crystalline-like relics → fast recrystallization.
- **Crystallization kinetics**: Trg = Tg/Tm ≈ 0.45–0.55 (marginal glass formers); timescale ratio of ~10¹⁶–¹⁷ between room-temperature stability and nanosecond switching at 250–550 °C.
- **Optical contrast origin**: primarily change in optical transition matrix elements (Fermi's golden rule), not just density or DOS changes. Lattice distortions reduce contrast.
- **Vacancies** on the Ge/Sb sublattice (~25% in Ge₁Sb₂Te₄) lower energy by reducing occupation of antibonding states — explained via p-orbital bonding model.
- **Material design rules**: average valence electrons > 4, octahedral structure, Trg ≈ 0.5, chalcogen + non-chalcogen in roughly equal proportions.
- Successful PCMs (GeTe, GST, IST, AIST) all have rocksalt/cubic structures; failures (AgInTe₂, AuInTe₂) have chalcopyrite structure with 4 valence electrons.
- Optical storage progression: CD-RW (780 nm, 650 MB) → DVD-RAM (650 nm, 4.7 GB) → BD-RE (405 nm, 23.3 GB); density ×100, data rate ×1000 over one decade.
- PCRAM: threshold switching enables practical operation; open questions (2007) include minimizing melt power and scaling below 20 nm.
