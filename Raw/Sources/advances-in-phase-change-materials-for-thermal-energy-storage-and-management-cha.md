## Abstract

The accelerating demand for high-performance thermal management in electronics and renewable energy systems has positioned phase change materials (PCMs) as critical components for passive temperature regulation due to their substantial latent heat, which absorbs the significant heat generated by electronic devices. This review offers a comprehensive analysis of PCMs, covering their selection criteria, classification, encapsulation techniques, and strategies for performance enhancement in thermal energy storage (TES) and thermal management systems in electronic devices. Despite significant advancements in PCM-based technologies, the incorporation of nanomaterials into PCMs to enhance thermal conductivity involves a series of interconnected trade-offs that lead to latent heat capacity reduction, leakage, viscosity, cyclic performance and durability, and physical and chemical stability that hinder their widespread adoption. In addition, this review presents a paradigm shift toward supramolecular phase change materials (SPCMs)an advanced class of materials that transcend the limitations of their traditional counterparts through the strategic exploitation of dynamic, noncovalent interactions, including hydrogen bonding and π–π stacking. These supramolecular architectures uniquely combine shape stabilization with emergent functionalities, such as self-healing capability, reprocessability, and exceptional dimensional stability, during phase transitions. We provide a systematic analysis of advanced modification and optimization strategies driving SPCM composite innovation. Key focuses include sophisticated encapsulation techniques, such as supramolecular lock shell layers, nanomaterials, nanoparticles, and porous matrix confinement, which achieve ultrahigh core loadings with exceptional cycling stability. Particular emphasis is placed on the strategic integration of functional nanofillers via surface chemical functionalization and noncovalent π–π stacking. These approaches uniquely overcome classical material trade-offs, simultaneously enhancing thermal conductivity and mechanical robustness while preserving latent heat capacityachievements unattainable with conventional PCMs. This review provides a focused roadmap for developing scalable, durable SPCMs tailored for next-generation thermal management applications.

## Phase-Change Materials

Phase change materials are substances that store and release large amounts of thermal energy during the process of melting and solidifying at a nearly constant temperature. When a PCM melts, it absorbs heat from the surroundings (endothermic process), and when it solidifies, it releases stored heat (exothermic process). ,

### Classification of Phase Change Materials Based on the Phase Transitions

PCMs could be categorized into solid–solid, solid–liquid, liquid–liquid, solid–gas, and liquid–gas PCMs based on the specific phase transitions they undergo. , (1) Solid–solid PCMs reveal small latent heat of phase transition, exhibit minimal volume changes, and are not recommended for actual thermal energy storage applications. Solid–solid phase transitions enhance the stability of PCMs, eliminating the need for support materials and significantly reducing leakage risks. Currently, there are two primary categories of solid–solid PCMs: polymer-based and polyol organic compound-based materials. (1) Polymer solid–solid-PCMs are typically synthesized by grafting crystallizable linear chains onto a polymer backbone. Examples include polyethylene glycol (PEG)/sorbitol, PEG/dextrose, and urethane-based compounds, which have been successfully developed for thermal energy storage. , (2) Polyol organic compounds, also referred to as plastic crystals, are easier to use but less cost-effective for large-scale industrial applications. Due to their inherent leakage resistance, solid–solid PCMs are especially well-suited for refrigerated packaging in the food and pharmaceutical industries, where even minor leaks are unacceptable. A chemically cross-linked solid–solid PCM (SSPCM) was developed, forming a robust 3D copolymer network that prevents leakage and imparts stability. By strategically balancing cross-linking via steric hindrance, the crystallinity of confined PEG segments was optimized, yielding a superior phase change enthalpy of 156.8 J/g. The material demonstrates exceptional shape stability (unchanged after 12 h at 120 °C), stable performance over 500 cycles, and a tensile strength of 6.6–11.0 MPa, outperforming comparable recyclable SSPCMs. (2) Solid–gas PCMs is a sublimation phase transition, and liquid–gas is an evaporation phase transition. They release the highest latent heat during change but are not considered because of the large volume change, and they need specialized applications to prevent material loss. (3) Solid–liquid PCMs have outstanding properties that balance between high latent heat of release/absorption and controlled volume change. These PCMs are the most useful, and several researchers have been attracted recently because they are hot research areas for TES. PCMs doped with thermal conductivity enhancers are referred to as composite PCMs (CPCMs). Among the various additives used, carbon-based materials are particularly popular in battery thermal management systems due to their high thermal conductivity, corrosion resistance, and low density. These properties enable efficient heat absorption and dissipation while also mitigating leakage issues commonly associated with liquid PCMs. Commonly employed carbon additives include expanded graphite, graphene, and carbon fiber, which enhance thermal performance without significantly increasing weight or compromising structural integrity. , Due to its capability to absorb and release energy from and in the environment, due to phase variation from solid to liquid state, when the temperature decreases, the materials can release the previously stored energy, passing from the liquid to the solid state, as illustrated in Figure .

## Encapsulation

Encapsulation is indeed a major and effective modification and optimization strategy for phase change materials. It is one of the most versatile and widely adopted modification strategies for PCMs. It transforms low-molecular-weight PCMs with leakage issues into stable, processable, and high-performance composites suitable for various applications. It plays a pivotal role in enhancing the performance and reliability of PCMs, particularly in addressing their inherent limitations, such as leakage during solid–liquid phase transitions. To overcome these challenges, researchers focus on optimizing nanoencapsulation techniques to ensure efficient, high-quality encapsulation of energy-rich materials. Two primary strategies have emerged to improve PCM stability and functionality: form stabilization and micro/nanoencapsulation. Form-stable PCMs are created by embedding the PCM within a micro- or macroscale supporting matrix, which physically contains the material while maintaining thermal efficiency. Alternatively, encapsulating PCMs within micro- or nanocapsules equipped with ‘smart shell’ properties offers multifaceted advantages, including controlled thermal energy release, prevention of environmental interaction, and protection against degradation during repeated heating/cooling cycles. Additionally, encapsulation enhances heat transfer by increasing the PCM’s effective surface area and thermal conductivity. These encapsulated PCMs can be processed into powders or pastes, enabling their integration into conventional construction materials, such as concrete, foam, and paint, to impart thermal energy storage capabilities. Beyond leakage prevention, encapsulation also improves long-term durability, scalability for industrial applications, and compatibility with multifunctional composites, making it a critical area of innovation for advanced thermal management systems. , The encapsulated PCMs can be microencapsulated phase change materials (MEPCMs) or nanoencapsulated phase change materials (NEPCMs), based on PCM lengths (MEPCMs: 0.1–1000 μm; NEPCMs: 1–100 nm). , These shells prevent PCM cores from entering the outside environment. The encapsulation is significant to keep the extraordinary surface area of PCM from sintering. Micro- or nanosized particles undergo faster heat transfer via their large surface area during charge/discharge between PCMs and the composites. Micro- or nanomaterials can exhibit diverse structures, such as tubular, spherical, and irregular. Further, the surface reform of PCMs during encapsulation is simple to obtain, which improves the possible application of PCMs.

### Optimizing and Modification of PCMs

Optimizing phase change materials requires a multifaceted approach that focuses on enhancing thermal conductivity, structural stability, and encapsulation efficiency. How does encapsulation modify and optimize PCMs? Encapsulation modifies and optimizes the PCMs by addressing the colleges such as leakage during phase transition, low thermal conductivity, corrosion and reactivity, volume expansion, poor cycling stability, contamination, and subcooling/supercooling. One of the primary limitations of PCMs is characteristically low thermal conductivity, which restricts their widespread application. To address this, researchers prioritize developing composite PCMs (CPCMs) with superior thermal conductivity and great energy storage density. By incorporating thermally conductive additives such as nanoparticles, expanded graphite, and metal foams, the heat transfer rate of PCMs can be significantly improved, leading to faster heat drive storage and release. Additionally, encapsulation techniques play a crucial role in preventing leakage and improving material stability. Beyond thermal conductivity, nanomaterials and porous additives further enhance CPCM performance by improving structural integrity over multiple thermal cycles. These additives offer advantages such as low density, high porosity, excellent shape-forming ability, and superior dispersion within the PCM matrix. Their large surface area-to-volume ratio not only boosts heat transfer efficiency but also reinforces encapsulation effectiveness, mitigating leakage issues commonly associated with PCMs. Moreover, the integration of these advanced materials ensures long-term durability and reliability, making CPCMs more viable for applications like building insulation, thermal energy storage systems, and electronics cooling. Future research could explore hybrid additive combinations or surface modifications to further optimize the thermal and mechanical properties, paving the way for next-generation energy storage and thermal management systems.

## Nucleon-Based Supramolecular Polymer

Supramolecular composite PCMs offer significant improvements over conventional PCMs in terms of thermal stability, leakage resistance, tunable properties, and energy storage efficiency, making them ideal for fuel cell thermal management. These advantages make them particularly suitable for thermal energy storage in fuel cells, where precise thermal management is crucial. Upcoming exploration should emphasize scaling up production and integrating these materials into real-world fuel cell systems. Supramolecular polymers have emerged from 30 years of combining polymer synthesis with noncovalent interactions, creating materials with dynamic, reversible assemblies. The development of stimulus-responsive supramolecular polymers based on nucleobases and oligomers has been investigated. These polymers readily self-assemble into nanoscale supramolecular micelles in aqueous solutions, exhibiting unique properties that make them highly promising as multifunctional materials for diverse applications. , It has enabled the realization of a diverse array of molecularly assembled materials and complex mechanically interlocked systems capable of, for example, switching behavior and even doing physical work. The fundamental physical, chemical, and mechanistic details of this field are based upon the design of intermolecular complexes that are created via specific noncovalent interactions (metal–ligand interactions, hydrogen bonding, π–π stacking, electrostatic interactions, and van der Waals forces). ,

Supramolecular Phase Change materials (SPCMs) are an advanced class where the phase change and energy storage are governed by dynamic, noncovalent interactions within a polymer network, such as hydrogen bonds or coordination bonds. In contrast, conventional PCMs utilize the latent heat absorbed or released during a physical state change, most commonly a solid–liquid transition, for thermal energy storage. This class includes organic materials like paraffins and fatty acids and inorganic materials such as salt hydrates. While effective for storing large amounts of heat, conventional PCMs face intrinsic challenges, such as liquid-phase leakage, low thermal conductivity, and property degradation over repeated cycles. ### Encapsulation of Nanomaterials with Supramolecular PCMs

In the context of supramolecular phase change materials (PCMs), encapsulation has emerged as a sophisticated modification strategy that involves confining the active phase change component within a protective shell or matrix to achieve shape stabilization and prevent leakage during solid–liquid transitions. A notable advancement in this area is the ″supramolecular lock shell layer″ technique, which utilizes intense noncovalent interactions particularly hydrogen bonding between the core material and the shell rather than relying on weak van der Waals forces or conventional surfactants. This approach facilitates the creation of stable nanoencapsulated PCMs (NC-PCMs) with ultrahigh core content, sometimes reaching up to 90 wt %, while maintaining excellent dispersion and stability across a wide temperature range. Another effective method is matrix encapsulation, where the PCM is impregnated into a 3D porous supporting material, such as a metal supramolecular gel. This strategy leverages capillary forces and surface tension to retain the PCM within the porous network, thereby ensuring shape stability even during repeated phase change cycles.

Modification strategies for supramolecular PCMs are diverse and focus on enhancing the performance while introducing new functionalities. A fundamental approach involves the chemical modification of the molecular building blocks themselves. For instance, researchers have developed solid–solid phase change coatings based on poly­(ethylene glycol) (PEG) functionalized with reactive silanol end groups. These groups form a self-cross-linking supramolecular network through multiple hydrogen bonds, effectively anchoring the PEG chains and enabling heat storage and release through reversible crystalline–amorphous transitions without liquefaction. Another significant modification pathway involves the creation of composite PCMs by the incorporation of functional fillers. For example, decorating supramolecular boron nitride nanosheets with catalytic silver nanoparticles yields a hybrid material with dual functionality, suitable for both thermal energy storage and photocatalytic applications.

Filler modification strategies play a pivotal role in tailoring supramolecular PCMs for specific applications. Surface chemical functionalization encompasses several approaches: carboxylation (−COOH) typically introduced via tartaric acid, amination (−NH2) using KH550 silane coupling agents, bis­(ureido)­butylene functionalization incorporating bis-urea moieties, and oligo­(β-alanine) attachment using β-alanine oligomers (unimer, dimer, and trimer) as functional groups. , Additionally, π–π stacking functionalization represents another important modification strategy. A notable example involves the noncovalent modification of single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) through π–π supramolecular interactions with triptycene-containing polyimide. The paddle-wheel-like triptycene moieties establish strong π–π stacking with the graphene walls of the nanotubes, effectively improving interfacial contact and enabling homogeneous dispersion without covalent modification, thereby preserving the intrinsic nanotube structure while enhancing compatibility. , Optimization strategies are crucial for tailoring supramolecular PCMs to specific applications and ensuring long-term performance stability. One primary optimization goal is enhancing thermal conductivity, which can be achieved by integrating the PCM with highly conductive porous networks, such as metal supramolecular gels, or by incorporating conductive nanomaterials into the composite matrix. Notably, supramolecular filler systems exhibit unique dynamic mechanical behavior compared to conventional fillers. Perhaps the most significant finding is that molecular-scale supramolecular fillers can overcome the classical trade-off between stiffness and toughness. Research has demonstrated that bis-urea fillers, when perfectly integrated into polymer hard segments, increase Young’s modulus by more than 100% without decreasing tensile strength or elongation at break. This contrasts sharply with conventional fillers or increasing the hard segment ratio, both of which invariably reduce toughness when increasing stiffness.

## Challenges in Phase Change Composite Material Development

The incorporation of nanomaterials into PCMs to enhance thermal conductivity involves a series of interconnected trade-offs leading to a decrease in the composite’s latent heat storage capacity, chemical incompatibility, high viscosity, short-term cyclic stability and cyclic durability, large supercooling due to the displacement of PCM mass, and potential confinement effects. Additionally, the revision will briefly address economic and environmental challenges, sustainability, and recyclability thereby offering a more holistic and decision-relevant perspective for researchers designing composite PCMs.

### Latent Heat Capacity Reduction

This is the most direct trade-off. The addition of thermally conductive but nonphase-changing nanomaterials dilutes the mass fraction of the active PCM, directly reducing the overall energy storage capacity (latent heat) per unit mass (specific latent heat) Table . Furthermore, nanofillers can physically confine the PCM molecules, restricting their mobility and inhibiting the complete phase transition, leading to an additional reduction in effective latent heat that is often greater than predicted by simple mixing rules. For example, the latent heat storage capacities were evaluated for both pure adipic acid (AA) and the resulting phase change composite (PCC). Pure AA demonstrated high latent heats of fusion (ΔHm) and solidification (ΔHs), measuring 202.0 and 201.2 J/g, respectively. In contrast, the AA/expanded graphite (EG) composite exhibited reduced values of 162.5 and 162.2 J/g for ΔHm and ΔHs. This decrease is attributed to the incorporation of EG, a material with no phase change enthalpy, which dilutes the overall latent heat capacity of the composite. However, the addition of EG significantly enhanced the thermal conductivity of the PCC compared to the insulating pure AA.

### Chemical and Physical Stability

While some nanomaterials can enhance structural stability, they can also introduce chemical incompatibility. Functional groups on nanofillers (e.g., oxidized graphene) may react with the PCM over time or during phase change cycles, leading to the degradation of either component. Poor interfacial bonding or agglomeration of nanoparticles can create microvoids or nonuniform composite structures, potentially causing leakage of molten PCM or reducing mechanical integrity, especially in form-stable composites. , To address the limitations, the researchers prepared a novel microtubule-encapsulated phase-change material (MTPCM) by infusing lauric acid (LA) into kapok fiber microtubules, which were precoated with silver nanoparticles. The LA/KF@Ag composite exhibited phase change temperatures of 44.1 °C (melting) and 42.1 °C (freezing) with a latent heat of fusion of 146.8 J/g. While the latent heat slightly decreased by 3.8% compared to the uncoated version, the addition of silver nanoparticles yielded a remarkable 92.3% increase in thermal conductivity. Consequently, LA/KF@Ag MTPCM demonstrated significantly enhanced thermal transfer efficiency, improving energy storage and release rates by 15.8% and 23.5%, respectively. To overcome the limitations of phase change materials, such as poor mechanical strength, low thermal conductivity, and a lack of recyclability. ### Viscosity and Processability

The addition of nanoparticles, especially at high loadings, dramatically increases the viscosity of the molten composite. This poses significant challenges for material processing, infiltration into porous matrices, and pumping in thermal fluid systems, impacting practical deployment and system design. The drastic increase in dynamic viscosity, particularly in the molten state, presents a major challenge for the system design and operation. The dispersion of high-aspect-ratio or high-surface-area nanomaterials (e.g., nanotubes and nanofibers) creates a networked structure that severely impedes fluid flow. This elevated viscosity hinders pumpability in active thermal management systems, reduces natural convection (negating one potential heat transfer mechanism), and complicates the manufacturing process itself, making it difficult to evenly infiltrate or cast the composite. , In the context of battery thermal management systems, employing a phase-change microencapsulated slurry presents a dual-sided efficacy. While the material’s high latent heat property significantly enhances the system’s cooling capacity, this advantage is partially counterbalanced by the slurry’s inherently high viscosity. The elevated viscosity imposes greater fluidic resistance, thereby increasing the pumping power required for circulation and, consequently, raising the overall energy consumption of the system during operation.

### Long-Term Cyclic Performance and Durability

The long-term performance is critically affected. The large difference in thermal expansion coefficients between the rigid nanomaterial and the PCM matrix can generate significant microscale stresses during repeated melting and freezing cycles. This can lead to degradation mechanisms such as particle aggregation or settling, which degrades thermal conductivity over time; interfacial debonding, creating thermal contact resistance; and micro- or nanocrack propagation within the matrix, ultimately causing structural failure and reduced cycle life. ,

To address this feedback, the design of supramolecular phase change materials, by incorporating linear PEG as the phase change functional component into a physically cross-linked network, which is formed by multivalent interactions in a linear supramolecular polyurethane, indicates the resultant SPCMs achieve a high latent heat of 143.1 J/g while maintaining solvent-assisted recyclability. Designing supramolecular phase change materials simultaneously possessing high latent heat and recyclability remains a significant challenge. The properties of these materials are highly tunable; both the latent heat (ranging from 87.3 to 143.1 J/g) and phase change temperature (36.4 to 59.8 °C) can be precisely adjusted by varying the molecular weight and content of the free PEG. Furthermore, the long-term cyclic performance and durability could be improved by incorporating single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNs) 2Wt % loading with a PEG/diatomite form-stable phase change composite, which were decorated onto the inner pore surfaces of the diatomite scaffold, and reports show a significant enhancement in the thermal conductivity, the composite increased dramatically from 0.24 W/m·K (pure PEG) to 0.87 W/m·K. Beyond technical performance, the widespread adoption of PCMs for thermal energy storage is critically constrained by environmental and economic challenges that impact their lifecycle sustainability and commercial viability. These challenges must be addressed holistically to transition from laboratory-scale innovation to responsible, large-scale deployment.

### Economic and Environmental Challenges

#### Economic Challenges:

Raw material expenses dominate for systems incorporating purified, functionalized nanomaterials or complex organic supramolecular gelators. Manufacturing complexity adds substantial cost; processes like interfacial polymerization for microencapsulation, freeze-drying, or sol gel synthesis are often batch-based, energy-intensive, and low-yield, contrasting sharply with the continuous, high-throughput processes required for industrial commoditization. This economic penalty must be justified by a system-level performance gain, such as dramatically faster charge/discharge rates enabling a smaller, more efficient heat exchanger rather than just a material-level property improvement.

#### Environmental Challenges:

The environmental profile of a PCM encompasses its entire lifecycle, from raw material sourcing to end-of-life disposal. Toxicity is a primary concern, particularly for certain salt hydrates containing heavy metals and for some synthetic organic PCMs, which may leach harmful substances upon degradation or in the event of encapsulation Furthermore, the production of high-performance nanocomposites often involves nanomaterials whose ecotoxicological effects are not fully understood, raising concerns about environmental persistence and bioaccumulation if released. A related and often overlooked challenge is recyclability. Most advanced PCM composites, especially those with intimately mixed nanomaterials or cross-linked polymer matrices, are designed for performance and not disassembly. This creates a ″monolithic waste″ problem at end-of-life, where separating valuable or hazardous components is economically and technically prohibitive, leading to downcycling or landfill disposal. ,

### Sustainability and Recyclability

The pursuit of sustainable thermal energy storage necessitates moving beyond performance metrics to a holistic evaluation of the environmental impact across the entire lifecycle of PCMs. For both emerging biobased and nanocomposite PCMs, explicit sustainability considerations that centered on life-cycle assessment and recyclability are critical to determine their true green credentials and guide responsible development. However, a comprehensive cradle-to-grave life-cycle assessment is required to quantify potential trade-offs, such as the land-use change, water consumption, and greenhouse gas emissions associated with their agricultural production and chemical processing, which may offset the benefits of biodegradability. for nanocomposite PCMs, the environmental burden is heavily front-loaded in the energy-intensive synthesis and functionalization of nanomaterials. A life-cycle assessment must account for the high embodied energy and potential toxicity of these fillers, comparing it against the operational energy savings gained from enhanced thermal conductivity over the product’s lifespan; without such analysis, a nanocomposite with superior performance may have a worse overall environmental footprint than a simpler, less conductive alternative. While the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into the design of PCMs offers transformative potential, accelerating the discovery of novel formulations and optimizing properties like latent heat and thermal conductivity, its practical implementation faces significant, often underexplored, hurdles that merit explicit discussion. A primary challenge is the data requirement: AI models, particularly deep learning and generative algorithms, demand extensive, high-quality, and well-labeled data sets encompassing synthesis parameters, structural characteristics, and thermophysical properties. Such comprehensive data sets are scarce in the specialized field of PCMs, where experimental data is frequently fragmented across studies with inconsistent reporting standards. This scarcity can lead to models that are overfitted to narrow data sets or lack generalizability. Furthermore, the black-box nature of many advanced AI models poses a validation challenge; predicting a promising material computationally is insufficient without a clear, interpretable rationale linking molecular structure to macroscopic performance, which is crucial for guiding experimental synthesis and establishing scientific trust. Therefore, a rigorous discussion must advocate for standardized data repositories and highlight the need for hybrid approaches that couple AI with physics-based simulations and high-throughput experimentation to create robust, closed-loop design frameworks. , Assessing the maturity and near-term deployability of PCM technologies necessitates the application of the Technology Readiness Level (TRL) framework, a systematic metric used to gauge the progression from basic research to commercial product. The fundamental science-basic to applied research (TRL1–3) is advanced and several niche products exist, system complete & deployment (TRL8–9), the major bottleneck lies in translating validated prototypes-technology development (TRL4–5) into reliable, large-scale systems-technology demonstration (TRL6–7). Key barriers at this stage include ensuring long-term stability over thousands of cycles, developing cost-effective and scalable manufacturing processes, and achieving seamless integration into existing energy or electronic systems. Future research must, therefore, pivot from pure material discovery toward focused engineering, lifecycle analysis, and system integration to bridge this gap and enable widespread commercial deployment.

## Thermal Conversion and Management in Electronics Devices

This review examines the role of CPCMs in thermal management systems for lithium-ion batteries with a focus on their potential for future advancements in supramolecular CPCM integration. Given the limited research specifically addressing CPCM applications in this field, this analysis provides a valuable and thorough evaluation. Lithium-ion batteries are widely used in portable electronics, including smartphones, laptops, digital cameras, e-cigarettes, and electric vehicles, due to their high energy density, extended lifespan, and eco-friendly nature. However, despite their commercial success, safety concerns such as fires and explosions have led to accidents, economic losses, and even fatalities. Research indicates that factors like short-circuiting, overcharging, physical damage, thermal runaway, and manufacturing flaws are major contributors to these failures., , Addressing these challenges is crucial for improving battery safety and performance. This section of the review addresses the critical challenge of heat dissipation in lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), which is managed through two primary approaches: internal and external battery thermal management systems (BTMS). Internal BTMS focuses on modifying the battery’s internal components to minimize heat generation at its source. Some researchers have proposed methods to modify the cathode and cathode materials and the electrode thickness. Key strategies include optimizing the electrode thickness and applying advanced surface coatings to the cathode. For instance, research demonstrates that while thinner electrodes reduce internal resistance, they can compromise energy density, necessitating a careful balance, as noted in studies on optimal thickness. Furthermore, surface modifications using carbon-based or oxide materials have been shown to enhance conductivity and reaction rates, although they may inadvertently reduce overall energy storage capacity. An alternative and promising internal strategy is enhancement of the battery separator. Advanced composite separators improve the thermal stability, ionic conductivity, and mechanical strength, contributing to safer and more manageable heat profiles. In parallel, External BTMS employs methods outside the battery cell, such as air, liquid, or PCM cooling systems which actively remove the heat that is generated. Together, these internal and external strategies form a comprehensive framework for managing LIB temperature, which is essential for ensuring battery safety, longevity, and performance. − PCMs are highly effective for battery cooling due to their substantial latent heat, which absorbs the significant heat generated by LIBs. By undergoing a phase change, PCMs maintain the battery pack at its optimal operating temperature. They are widely considered the most efficient cooling technology because they are low-cost, easy to install, and offer high cooling efficiency, which also helps achieve a uniform temperature distribution across cells. However, the intrinsic low thermal conductivity of pure PCM limits their ability to manage temperature during prolonged battery use. This key limitation can be addressed by incorporating thermal conductivity enhancers, such as carbon- or metal-based additives, to form CPCMs. The enhanced thermal conductivity allows heat to be dissipated more effectively to the environment, delaying thermal runaway and significantly improving overall temperature controllability.

Researchers have introduced thermal conductivity enhancers, such as carbon-based or metallic additives, that enhance heat transfer and help prevent thermal runaway. For example, Zou et al. examined the effects of multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) and graphene in PCM-based thermal management systems. By incorporating just 1% of these additives, they increased the thermal conductivity by 41% and 61.5%, respectively. Further optimization was achieved by blending MWCNTs and graphene in varying ratios, with a 30:70 mix yielding a remarkable 123.1% improvement in thermal conductivity. This optimized CPCM maintained battery temperatures below 46 °C. In a follow-up study, the same team evaluated a composite PCM made from paraffin, expanded graphite (EG), graphene, and CNTs, demonstrating its effectiveness in battery thermal management. Similarly, other studies have explored the use of nanomaterials, including carbon nanotubes (CNTs) and metal nanoparticles, to further improve the PCM thermal conductivity. Karimi et al. investigated the impact of silver (Ag), copper (Cu), and iron oxide (Fe3O4) nanoparticles in PCMs and found that all three significantly enhanced performance compared to pure PCM. Among them, Ag nanoparticles proved most effective, reducing temperature differences in battery cells by 50%. Lastly, the designed composite PCM cooling plates, by combining paraffin with graphene for use in prismatic cells, were investigated. Their tests revealed that CPCM cooling was more effective than traditional liquid cooling, lowering cell temperatures from 56.5 to 36.5 °C during a 4C discharge (where 1C denotes the current required to fully discharge a battery in 1 h) with 6 mm thick CPCM plates.

Composite phase change materials (PW-MXene/CNFs@MoS2) with hierarchical multiinterfacial architecture are developed to synergistically manage thermal shock and electromagnetic interference. The material combines efficient phonon conduction (121.8 J/g enthalpy, 97% retention after 300 cycles) with effective electromagnetic wave absorption, demonstrated by a 6.4 °C thermal buffer under 1200 W/m2 and Bluetooth signal shielding, offering a promising solution for advanced electronics. A multidimensional composite phase change material (PW@MXene/CoNi–C) was developed by integrating 2D MXene, 1D CNTs, and 0D Co/Ni nanoparticles. The material synergistically combines ultrahigh photothermal conversion (97.5% efficiency) and strong microwave absorption (−49.3 dB at 8.03 GHz). As a flexible film, it enables integrated thermal management, electromagnetic shielding, photothermal therapy, and electrical signal detection, offering a versatile platform for advanced wearable electronics. Using a bionic strategy, a neural-network-like aerogel (CNF@Co/C) was developed to encapsulate paraffin wax (PW), forming a multifunctional composite phase change material. This PW-CNF@Co/C composite achieves high-performance solar-thermal conversion (95.27% efficiency) via synergistic plasmonic Co/C and CNF photon transport and effective microwave absorption (−26.8 dB minimum reflection loss). The integrated design offers prospects for thermal and electromagnetic management in compact electronic devices operating in variable outdoor conditions.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, supramolecular phase change materials (PCMs) represent a transformative advancement in thermal energy storage, particularly for applications such as battery thermal management, where high efficiency, system simplicity, and enhanced durability are paramount. Through sophisticated encapsulation strategiesincluding supramolecular lock shell layers and porous matrix confinementresearchers have successfully reconciled high latent heat capacity with excellent cycling stability, achieving shape-stabilized systems with ultrahigh core content and exceptional dimensional integrity during phase transitions. The strategic incorporation of functional fillers via surface chemical functionalization (e.g., carboxylation, amination, bis-urea moieties, and oligo­(β-alanine) attachment) and noncovalent π–π stacking approaches has enabled the creation of multifunctional composites that overcome classical material trade-offs, such as the balance between thermal conductivity and energy density or the stiffness-toughness paradox. These supramolecular systems uniquely achieve simultaneous increases in modulus and ductility while offering additional advantages including reduced rolling resistance and high self-healing efficiency. By systematically addressing persistent limitationslow thermal conductivity, heat loss, and long-term stabilitythrough molecular engineering and thermodynamic control, this review synthesizes previously fragmented knowledge to provide a targeted guide for future research. The ultimate goal is to accelerate the development of scalable, cost-effective, and reliable supramolecular PCMs that can deliver more efficient and sustainable energy management solutions for next-generation technologies.
