Thermally induced nanoscale phase change in chalcogenide glass Cr2Ge2Te6 revealed by scanning tunneling microscopy 
 Dang-il Kim1, Mizuki Kawaji1, Hiroki Sato1, Ryota Kawamura1, Ryo Tamaki1, Satoshi Kusaba1, Yinli Wang2, Yi Shuang3, Yuji Sutou2, 3, Ikufumi Katayama1 and Jun Takeda1

Cr2Ge2Te6 (CrGT) is viewed as an important phase change material (PCM) for next generation nonvolatile memory devices because of its superior properties, e.g., high thermal stability and low operation energy, compared to conventional PCMs. However, the phase change mechanism of CrGT remains unsolved, especially at the nanoscale. Here, we investigated thermally induced nanoscale phase changes of CrGT thin films using scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) and scanning tunneling spectroscopy (STS). By performing a statistical analysis of measured STM topographic and STS data, we evaluated the inhomogeneity and distribution of the phase change characteristics of CrGT thin films. We also related the nanoscale phase change properties of CrGT to macroscopic phase changes by comparing the STM and STS results with experimental data from Raman spectroscopy.  1. Introduction 
Phase change materials (PCMs) have attracted considerable attention for the development of phase change random access memory (PCRAM), which could be used in next generation nonvolatile memory devices exhibiting fast access times, high endurance and low operation power [1-3]. To date, many different types of PCMs have been investigated, including a typical Ge-Sb-Te based chalcogenide compound, Ge2Sb2Te5 (GST) [4,5]. However, some properties of GST, such as poor thermal stability and high operation energy for phase switching between amorphous and crystalline phases, hamper its application in high temperature environments or when a low operation energy at limited power supply is desired. To solve these drawbacks, nitrogen or transition metal doped PCMs have been fabricated to improve the resistivity in the crystalline phase, resulting in improvement of the reset operation energy and increased thermal stability [6-9].  
Recently, Ge-Te based alloys have been synthesized with improved thermal stability by

doping with transition metals, such as Cr and Cu [10]. As a result, Cr2Ge2Te6 (CrGT) has catapulted to the forefront as a possible candidate for future PCRAM, and its phase change properties have been explored recently [11]. Compared to conventional PCMs, CrGT shows inverse resistance characteristics, i.e. a high-resistance crystalline reset state and low-resistance amorphous set state, caused by a drastic decrease in carrier density upon crystallization. Further, CrGT has additional superior properties to GST, i.e., a high crystallization temperature above 270 ℃ and very low crystallization energy [12,13]. These characteristics have been investigated through macroscopic measurements using as-deposited CrGT films, CrGT with a tungsten electrode and a CrGT memory cell fabricated by a conventional photolithography technique. However, nanoscopic observation of the amorphous-to-crystalline phase change accompanying the variation of electronic states is still out of reach, despite its significance.  
In this work, we investigated nanoscale phase changes of CrGT thin films using scanning tunneling microscopy (STM). STM is a powerful technique for probing a material’s surface structure with atomic resolution by applying a bias voltage between a tip and target sample while scanning the tip position over the surface [14-17]. Furthermore, by sweeping the bias voltage of the STM at a fixed tip position on the sample, i.e. implementing scanning tunneling spectroscopy (STS), it is possible to measure the differential conductance of the sample, which is proportional to the density of states (DOS) of the material [18-20]. STM has previously been utilized on the typical phase change material GST to induce a nanoscale phase change either by applying voltage pulses through the STM tip or introducing laser pulses to the STM junction [21-23]. In the present study, we conducted STM and STS measurements with increasing annealing temperature to investigate thermally induced nanoscale amorphous-to-crystalline phase changes of CrGT thin films. 
Since the amorphous phase corresponded to inhomogeneity in the dataset of the measurements, we employed a statistical method to analyze random distributions of the phase change. We detected a decrease in the differential conductance and change in inhomogeneity during the crystallization process at the nanoscale. The results were compared with macroscopic Raman spectroscopy measurements to evaluate the correlation between the nanoscopic and macroscopic properties of the phase change. The method used could facilitate the characterization and development of future phase change memory devices using CrGT. 
 2. Experimental Method 
A commercial ultra-high vacuum (UHV) STM was used with a base pressure of approximately 5×10-8 Pa. The STM probe tip was fabricated by electrochemical etching in a 
NaOH solution using a polycrystalline tungsten wire of diameter φ = 0.5 mm. The tip was 
mounted on the STM head stage and cleaned by applying a high bias voltage pulse several times

on an atomically flat gold thin film. All the STM experiments were conducted under ambient temperature conditions because PCRAMs should ideally be operated at room temperature. To obtain a surface suitable for STM and STS measurements, a CrGT film was deposited on a cleaved highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) substrate. The CrGT film was deposited using a magnetron co-sputtering method under vacuum conditions of 0.5 Pa by controlling the Radio Frequency (RF) power of Cr, Ge and Te targets so that the film composition was a nearly stoichiometric composition of Cr2Ge2Te6. The film thickness was approximately 100 nm. After film deposition, the samples were dipped into deionized water for 1 minute to remove the oxidized layer on the chalcogenide thin film [24]. After loading the samples into the UHV chamber, the samples were annealed at 200 ℃, far below to the crystallization temperature (270 ℃ [11]), to form stable amorphous phase CrGT films. Note that this annealing temperature still maintain the electronic properties of amorphous phase CrGT, although the annealed sample begins to form slightly the short-range bonding order at around 200 ℃. Until completion of a set of STM and STS experiments, the CrGT thin films were stored in the UHV chamber to avoid sample oxidation and contamination. 
A schematic of this work is shown in Fig. 1. As shown in the lower part of Fig. 1, the bonding of CrGT was modified along with a thermally induced amorphous-to-crystalline phase change via a metastable state [11], whose modification was tracked by STM and STS measurements. We first measured an STM topographic image of the amorphous phase CrGT film 
under a bias voltage of Vs = 1 V and setpoint of Is = 5-10 pA. Judging from the stable detection of 
the topographic image in a conventional manner, we confirmed that the surface quality of the CrGT film was suitable for the experiments. Next, a set of STM measurements, i.e., STM images and STS data, was acquired for films prepared at different annealing temperatures from 200 to 320 ℃. The CrGT thin film was annealed at a given temperature during 3 hours in the UHV chamber, and then the experiments were carried out in the same chamber after 1 hour of cooling. The annealing temperature was monitored by a radiation thermometer (KTL-PRO). In a previous study, it was reported that the conductivity of amorphous phase CrGT is affected by several factors, such as localized in-gap states, variable range hopping and the percolation effect, whereas the conduction mechanism in the crystalline phase is sensitive to nearest neighbor hopping [25]. Since 
these effects may yield a fluctuating differential conductance (dI/dV) spectrum depending on the 
tip-sample location even under the same experimental conditions, we carefully conducted STS 
measurements to ensure reproducibility; i.e. the dI/dV spectrum was averaged over several times 
at each sample position and then a histogram was constructed from the data obtained at 2500 points in a 500 × 500 nm2 area. Since the valence band maximum (VBM) of CrGT is known to decrease after crystallization [13], the difference in electronic properties between the amorphous 
and crystalline phases was investigated by measuring the dI/dV spectrum. We first swept the bias

Finally, STM topographic images were reprocessed and a roughness analysis was conducted using WSxM software [26].  3. Results and Discussion 
Figure 2(a) shows STM topographic images of films annealed at various temperatures, while Fig. 2(b) depicts line profiles in (a) at positions indicated by the black lines. After annealing the as-deposited sample at 200 ℃, the topography of the amorphous CrGT film showed a relatively flat surface comprising many grains with diameters of about 15-25 nm. As the annealing temperature was increased from 200 to 320 ℃, the surface morphology gradually changed and the grain size became larger. After the annealing temperature exceeded the crystallization point, the grain size increased further, eventually forming large grains with diameters of 50-60 nm at 320 ℃. The grain growth was not only in the in-plane direction but also normal to the surface, as evident by the height change in the line profiles of the STM images. Based on the line profiles, the root mean square (RMS) surface roughness was estimated as 0.87, 0.91, 5.5 and 7.8 nm at 200, 250, 290 and 320 ℃, respectively. As the results suggested that the amorphous-to-crystalline phase change of CrGT was accompanied by fluctuation or inhomogeneity of the surface topography, a statistical analysis was conducted to evaluate the inherent characteristics of the nanoscale phase change of CrGT. 
The roughness of the CrGT film was investigated by power spectral density (PSD) analysis using WSxM software [26]. PSD analysis is often used for the statistical evaluation of topographic patterns that contain surface roughness, e.g., SEM images of photoresist materials and laser craters formed in zinc crystals [27,28]. Images from scanning probe microscopy (SPM), including STM and atomic force microscopy (AFM), have also been evaluated by PSD analysis, e.g., roughness evaluation of nanostructures in self-assembled organic acids and nanoscale dust 
on a glass [29,30]. In the present study, the lateral correlation length x and vertical roughness σ 
were calculated using PSD after performing two-dimensional fast Fourier transformation (2D-
FFT) of the measured STM topographic data shown in Fig. 2 [27]. The correlation length x represents the length how frequently the same height in an STM image appears and was 
estimated as 0.30, 0.77, 1.0 and 1.8 nm at 200, 250, 290 and 320 ℃, respectively, then σ was 
calculated using WSxM as 0.55, 0.72, 4.5 and 8.3 nm at 200, 250, 290 and 320 ℃, respectively. The trend in roughness was consistent with the single-line analysis shown in Fig. 2. According 
to these analyses, the values of both x and σ increased with increasing annealing temperature 
and changed considerably near the crystallization point. The results indicated that a structural change occurred simultaneously in-plane and out-of-plane of the CrGT film. We also carried out the XRD mesurement and then found that the (003), (006) and (0012) diffraction signals emerged

with increasing the annealing temperature, indicating that the amorphous CrGT film underwent 
nanoscale crystallization along the c-axis, i.e., approximately perpendicular to the substrate 
[11,12].  To confirm the relation between the nanoscale crystallization and phase change behavior, and evaluate the variation in electronic structure with spatial distribution, STS was conducted 
on CrGT films for different annealing temperatures. Figure 3 shows current–voltage (I-V) and dI/dV characteristics of CrGT films annealed at 200 and 320 ℃, corresponding to amorphous 
and crystalline phases, respectively. A considerable current reduction was observed in the negative bias region after annealing at 320 ℃, implying a deficiency in the hole carrier density 
after crystallization. In addition, the dI/dV spectra of the CrGT film showed semiconductor-like behavior: i.e., near-zero value of dI/dV at around the zero bias voltage. From the width of the near-zero region of dI/dV, we concluded that band-gap opening occurred after annealing at 
320 ℃. In previous studies, the band-gap of amorphous CrGT was evaluated as 0.61 eV, whereas that of the crystalline phase was estimated to be 0.91 eV [31]. Our results are consistent with previous research showing that the carrier density of a CrGT film is reduced when the annealing temperature exceeds the crystallization temperature [13]. Due to its unique phase change property originating from the presence of Cr nanoclusters and vacancies [32], when CrGT undergoes crystallization, the proportion of Cr nanoclusters reduces alongside their rearrangement to form Cr–Te bonding after trapping excess Cr atoms into vacancies [33]. During this crystallization process, localized electrons in Cr nanoclusters are redistributed into Cr–Te bonding, decreasing the hole carrier density. This electron delocalization contributes to a 
reduction of the DOS in the negative bias region, which can be detected in the dI/dV spectrum. 
Next, we explored the fluctuation or inhomogeneity of the electronic states accompanying 
the amorphous-to-crystalline phase change using dI/dV mapping measured over 50 × 50 divided points in a 500 × 500 nm2 area. From the mapping results in Fig. 4(a), histograms of dI/dV at -0.8 
V were plotted for different annealing temperatures and fitted to Gaussian profiles, as shown in Fig. 4(b). At low annealing temperatures, the distribution was widely dispersed, probably due to the amorphous nature of the sample. With increasing annealing temperature, the Gaussian 
distribution became narrower and the dI/dV value decreased, indicating an increase of crystallized 
domains. 
Finally, to compare the observed nanoscale phase changes with macroscopic experimental results, we measured a Raman spectrum for each annealed sample using a conventional microscopic Raman spectrometer (inVia reflex) with 532 nm excitation wavelength 
and 1.5 µm lateral spatial resolution. Figure 5(a) shows Raman spectra of CrGT thin films prepared with different annealing temperatures. For comparison, we also measured the Raman spectrum of a commercially available CrGT crystal (purchased from 2D semiconductors) under

the same experimental conditions. The as-deposited film did not show clear Raman peaks between 100 to 200 cm-1, whereas Raman peaks appeared slightly at 110 and 135 cm-1 after annealing at 200 ℃, corresponding to Eg 
2 and Ag 1 modes, respectively [34]. As the annealing temperature 
increased, the Raman peaks became more pronounced and other weak peaks, e.g., corresponding to Eg 
3, Eg 4 and Ag 
2 modes, emerged. Based on the similarity to the Raman spectrum of single crystalline CrGT, we concluded that the annealing of CrGT thin films stimulated the growth of crystalline domains.  
For quantitative comparison of the phase change behavior observed from nanoscopic and macroscopic measurements, we plotted the fraction of crystalline CrGT estimated from STS and Raman measurements as a function of the inverse of the annealing temperature, as shown in Fig. 5(b). The fraction of crystalline CrGT is defined by the two experiments as follows. In the case of the macroscopic phase change observed by Raman measurement, the fraction of crystalline CrGT was evaluated from ratio of the Raman peak intensity of the annealed CrGT film sample relative to that of single crystalline CrGT, as shown by the open squares and closed triangles for the Eg 
2 and Ag 
1 peaks, respectively. In the case of the nanoscopic phase change observed via dI/dV 
histograms (Fig. 4b), we assumed that a value of dI/dV lower than 90 pA/V represented the 
nanoscopic crystalline characteristic of CrGT, whereas a value higher than 160 pA/V represented the amorphous characteristic (the values 90 pA/V and 160 pA/V corresponded to the center of the Gaussian distribution at 320 and 200 ℃, respectively). Next, we estimated the fraction of crystalline CrGT by calculating the ratio of the histogram’s count below 90 pA/V to the summation of that below 90 pA/V and above 160 pA/V for each annealing temperature, as shown by the closed circles in Fig. 5(b). With increasing annealing temperature, the fraction of crystalline CrGT increased according to both the STS and Raman measurements, strongly indicating that the nanoscale phase changes observed by STM and STS measurements were accompanied by macroscopic phase changes of CrGT. In addition, the results were well fitted by the Arrhenius law, as shown by the red solid line in Fig. 5, suggesting that the thermally induced amorphous-to-crystalline phase change in the CrGT film required an activation energy of around 3.0 eV. This indicated that the CrGT film had a higher thermal stability in the amorphous phase than the GST film [12]. Therefore, by conducting macroscopic measurements in conjunction with statistical analysis of nanoscopic measurements, it was possible to evaluate the thermally induced amorphous-to-crystalline phase change of CrGT films.  4. Conclusion 
Our results demonstrated a thermally induced nanoscale phase change in CrGT films by utilizing STM and STS. We identified a surface morphology change involving grain growth with increasing annealing temperature and detected differences in the electronic states between the

 7 
amorphous and crystalline phases in CrGT thin films. Based on PSD analysis of two-dimensional FFT STM topographic data, we evaluated the inhomogeneity and distribution of the phase change characteristics of CrGT thin films. Furthermore, by comparing STS and Raman spectroscopy results, we correlated the nanoscale and macroscale phase changes of CrGT. We believe that the results of this study will be useful for further understanding the phase change mechanism of CrGT at the nanoscale and its future application as higher storage density phase change memory devices.
