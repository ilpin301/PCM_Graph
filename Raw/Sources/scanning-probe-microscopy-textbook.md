# Scanning Probe Techniques — Principles, Instrumentation, and Atomic Manipulation

Scanned textbook excerpt ("Buchseiten") on scanning probe microscopy (SPM). Extracted to a NotebookLM study-guide companion (in Russian); key claims below are the factual content in English.

## Key claims

- **SPM** is a family of surface-science techniques with **atomic resolution**. Unlike scattering experiments, SPM produces **real-space** images. A sharp probe is scanned at a small distance over the surface; the tip–surface interaction (electrical, magnetic, or mechanical) is measured and converted into a signal. The field began in **1981** with the invention of the scanning tunneling microscope (STM).
- **STM** uses the **quantum-mechanical tunneling effect** to image **conducting** surfaces (metals, semiconductors, superconductors). A tunneling current `I` arises when a bias is applied between a metal tip and the sample at a gap of ~1 nm; the current depends **exponentially** on the gap `z`: `I ∼ exp(−2 k_eff z)`. A 0.1 nm change in distance produces a large current change, enabling atomic resolution.
- **STM images the local density of electronic states (LDOS) near the Fermi energy**, not the atoms directly.
- STM operating modes: **constant-current** (feedback adjusts tip height to keep the current constant) and **constant-height** (current variations are recorded at a fixed height).
- Clean STM surfaces require **ultra-high vacuum**, pressure < 1×10⁻⁸ Pa.
- **AFM / scanning force microscopy (SFM)** measures **forces** rather than current, so it can image **insulators, organic, and biological** samples. Relevant forces: van der Waals, electrostatic, magnetic, capillary, and repulsive (contact). The **Hamaker constant `A`** is the material-specific parameter setting the van der Waals force.
- AFM detection uses the **optical-lever method**: a laser beam reflects off the back of the cantilever onto a photodiode to track deflection.
- AFM variants: **MFM** (magnetic force microscopy — magnetic probe, e.g. data-storage media), **EFM** (electrostatic force microscopy — surface charge distribution), **PFM** (piezoresponse force microscopy — ferroelectrics).
- Soft biological samples are protected via **tapping (intermittent-contact) mode** or **Q-control** in the feedback loop, working in the low-interaction-force regime.
- **Atomic manipulation** enables **bottom-up nanotechnology**: **lateral manipulation** (dragging/pushing an atom along the surface without lifting it) and **vertical manipulation** (transferring particles between surface and tip apex).
- **Quantum corral**: an artificial nanostructure (e.g. 48 Fe atoms on Cu) assembled atom-by-atom to confine a 2D electron gas and visualize the electrons' wave nature.
- **Controlled chemistry**: the STM tip can induce and stepwise-control reactions, e.g. the **Ullmann reaction** (aryl coupling, biphenyl synthesis) at low temperature.
- **Piezo tubes** provide precise 3D tip motion (x, y, z).
